Today is about one month and one day after Google launched the March 2024 core and spam updates. Google mentioned that the core update would take up to a month to roll out, and while it feels like we are at the tail end, Google has not officially confirmed that it is complete yet. It’s not uncommon for these updates to take longer than initially stated, especially when there are many adjustments and refinements happening during the rollout.

One of the biggest questions in the SEO industry right now is why sites impacted by the September 2023 Helpful Content Update have not shown signs of recovery. From analyzing a large dataset of sites—often in the thousands—there has been little to no positive movement. Most sites remain flat or continue to decline in visibility and traffic, which is surprising given that Google has previously suggested improvements could be seen within a few months.

The Helpful Content System itself was introduced in 2022 but had a much larger and more noticeable impact during the September 2023 update. Many sites experienced severe traffic losses, in some cases losing up to 95% of their organic traffic. Google has been clear that sites with a higher proportion of unhelpful content would be more significantly impacted, and this seems consistent with what has been observed.

From an agency perspective, especially working with enterprise and mid-sized brands across industries like healthcare, finance, and e-commerce, these types of sites were generally not affected by the Helpful Content Update in the same way. While they may experience fluctuations during core updates, they were not devastated like many niche or affiliate sites. This highlights a growing divide between large, established brands and smaller content-driven websites.

SEO has always been cyclical, and similar patterns have been observed in past algorithm updates. For example, the Penguin update in 2012 targeted manipulative link-building practices, and the Medic update in 2018 significantly impacted health-related websites by emphasizing expertise, authority, and trust. In each case, sites that relied on risky or borderline tactics saw strong short-term gains followed by severe long-term losses, often requiring years to recover.

A major issue with many of the sites affected by the Helpful Content Update is that they tend to look very similar to one another. They often follow the same SEO playbooks, produce similar types of content, and lack unique value or perspective. Even when the content is written by humans, it can still feel generic and unoriginal, offering little that differentiates it from other pages on the internet.

Google’s recent changes also appear to be influenced by broader criticism of search quality. Over the past few years, there has been increasing concern that search results have become saturated with low-quality, SEO-driven content. Articles and discussions online have pointed out that users often add “Reddit” to their searches to find more authentic and experience-based information. In response, Google has been placing more emphasis on firsthand experience and rewarding platforms like Reddit and Quora, which naturally contain user-generated insights.

The introduction of “experience” into the E-E-A-T framework reflects this shift. Google now expects content creators to demonstrate real-world knowledge, not just summarize information from other sources. For example, in product reviews, simply listing features and specifications is no longer enough. Content needs to show that the author has actually used the product, with specific details, personal insights, and practical observations.

One important reason why recovery has not yet occurred is timing. Historically, recovery from major algorithm updates can take a long time. Based on past experience with updates like Penguin and Medic, it can take anywhere from one to three years to see meaningful recovery, even when significant improvements are made. Additionally, Google may not have fully processed all the changes made to affected sites yet, as crawling and indexing can take time, especially if site visibility has already declined.

Despite the challenges, there are some positive signs. Certain types of sites, such as recipe blogs and niche publishers, have started to recover during the March 2024 update. A common pattern among these sites is that they go beyond just publishing basic content. They often have additional elements such as real businesses, strong brand identities, social media presence, or diverse content formats like videos and community engagement.

The key takeaway is that simply improving existing content is not enough. Site owners need to rethink their entire approach, focusing on creating genuinely helpful, experience-driven content and building a recognizable brand. This includes adding unique perspectives, sharing real experiences, and offering value that cannot be easily replicated by other sites.

Ultimately, while the situation is difficult and frustrating for many site owners, especially those who have lost significant traffic and revenue, the path forward involves long-term commitment and substantial effort. Recovery is possible, but it requires patience, consistency, and a shift away from purely SEO-driven strategies toward more authentic and user-focused content creation.
