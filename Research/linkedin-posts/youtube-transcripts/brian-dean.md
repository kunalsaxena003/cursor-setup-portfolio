#Transcript
In this video, I’m going to show you how I started a side project that turned into a business that eventually sold for millions. And today, I’m going to show you exactly how I did it and the most important lessons that you can apply to your own business.

Looking back, the number one thing that made a difference was that I took advantage of an opportunity, but not a fad. Before I started my first business, an SEO training company called Backlinko, I struggled with online business. The main reason, looking back, was that I kept jumping from shiny object to shiny object. Whether it was affiliate marketing or something else, it was always a new platform, a new tactic, a new strategy. I was constantly switching instead of focusing on a real opportunity in the market and committing to it long term.

If you want to build a business that is actually successful and legitimate, you usually need to work on it for at least five years. You can do okay in a shorter time, and it does happen, but most businesses take years to grow into something significant. I was tired of chasing side hustles that never went anywhere. I wanted to build something real.

I realized there was an opportunity for a platform that teaches white hat SEO with actionable strategies. At that time, most SEO advice was vague. It talked about creating content and building relationships but didn’t explain exactly what to do. I created the site that I wanted myself, knowing it would take time to grow.

I was willing to work on it for five years. It wasn’t about jumping on a temporary trend. It was about committing to something meaningful. You should find an opportunity in a growing market, but also something you’re willing to work on for years. If you don’t enjoy it, you won’t stick with it.

Most gurus promote jumping on the next big thing because they need content. It’s easier to sell quick success than long-term effort. But real entrepreneurs consistently say the same thing: they built something and worked on it for years. That’s the reality of building a valuable business.

Another important factor was delayed gratification. When I launched Backlinko, I didn’t monetize it for about 10 months. Instead, I focused entirely on building an audience. Not monetizing early allowed me to understand my audience deeply. People told me exactly what they needed, and that shaped my product.

When I finally created my course, it wasn’t generic. It was a step-by-step system because that’s what my audience asked for. If I had created a product earlier based on assumptions, it likely wouldn’t have worked as well.

Another key lesson was focusing on one channel at a time. It’s tempting to try everything at once—Instagram, YouTube, LinkedIn, podcasts—but that approach usually leads to mediocre results. I focused first on SEO and blog content. Once that gained traction, I moved to email. Then later to YouTube and other platforms.

Each channel took time to learn and master. I didn’t know how to build an email list or create videos when I started. I learned step by step. Trying to do everything at once would have slowed down progress across all channels.

Learning how to sell through email was another major turning point. Almost all sales came from email, not from website buttons or passive traffic. Email allows direct communication with your audience, making it much more effective for selling.

When I launched products through email, results were significantly faster. Instead of waiting months for sales, I could generate strong revenue quickly because I had an engaged audience ready to buy.

At some point, I hit a plateau. Growth slowed, and I didn’t know how to move forward. A friend introduced me to the concept of problem-aware and problem-expert audiences. Problem experts actively seek solutions and are easier to sell to. Problem-aware people know they have an issue but don’t see it as urgent.

Initially, I was only targeting problem experts. Once that group was saturated, growth stalled. To move forward, I had to learn how to educate problem-aware people and show them why their problem mattered. This required a different marketing approach, especially through pre-launch content that builds urgency and awareness.

Finally, one of the biggest lessons was focusing on compound channels instead of one-off strategies. Compound channels like SEO, email, and YouTube build momentum over time. Each piece of content adds to your overall growth.

In contrast, channels like paid ads require constant effort without building lasting assets. With compound channels, you’re creating something that grows and becomes more valuable over time. This also makes your business more sellable because you’re building real assets like an audience, content, and brand.
